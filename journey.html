E-Lab journey

Our five challenges

01

Challenge 1: Introduction

What we did: Introduced ourselves as a team spanning six different countries, mapping our different backgrounds, cultures, and experiences against the one shared mission we'd be working toward for the rest of the E-Lab.

What we learned: Six nations, one mission — our differences turned out to be a strength, giving us a wider range of perspectives to draw on from day one.

▶ Watch on YouTube
02

Challenge 2: Discover Africa — Queen Pokou

What we did: Researched and presented the story of Queen Pokou of the Baoulé people, one African community's history of leadership and sacrifice.

What we learned: Leadership, Sacrifice, and a story too important to forget — Queen Pokou's willingness to give up everything for her people is a reminder that the region's history holds lessons in accountability we still draw on today.

▶ Watch on YouTube
03

Challenge 3: HELP-LAB

What we did: Volunteered our time helping students at a local school, supporting lessons and offering mentorship alongside our regular E-Lab work.

What we learned: Working directly with students grounded our project in real community needs and reminded us why accessible, offline-friendly education matters.

▶ Watch on YouTube
04

Challenge 4: Hunt for Treasure

What we did: Interviewed a member of parliament to gain firsthand information and experience on how the DRC's natural resources are governed and managed.

What we learned: Hearing directly from a policymaker showed us the gap between resource policy on paper and what actually reaches citizens on the ground — insight that shaped our problem statement.

▶ Watch on YouTube

The team has a YouTube video for this challenge.

05

Challenge 5: Our Solution — CongoSkills

What we did: Designed and presented CongoSkills, our proposed solution — an offline-first training and transparency platform addressing the DRC's poverty and accountability challenges.

What we learned: A viable solution has to work without reliable internet, and it has to give citizens — not just institutions — a real way to hold power accountable.
